Where You're Born Matters

Life expectancy varies dramatically across the world. Africa averages 62.47 years, while Europe exceeds 79 years â€” a gap of nearly two decades.

The Geography of Longevity

Our choropleth map reveals stark geographic patterns. The darkest regions â€” concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa â€” represent countries where average life expectancy remains below 55 years.

In contrast, Japan (83.8 years), Switzerland (82.9 years), and Iceland (82.5 years) lead the world with life expectancies exceeding 82 years.

Key Insight

Developed nations average 78.51 years vs 66.34 years for developing nations â€” a 12.17-year gap determined largely by birth location.

Regional Variance

The box plot shows Africa has both the lowest median (62.47 years) AND highest variance in outcomes, indicating significant inequality even within the continent.

02 â€” Socioeconomic Factors

Does Money Buy Time?

GDP per capita and education levels show strong correlations with life expectancy, but the relationship is more nuanced than simple wealth accumulation.

The Preston Curve

Our scatter plot reveals the famous Preston Curve relationship. Yes, wealth correlates with longevity â€” but with diminishing returns. The curve flattens dramatically after $20,000 GDP per capita.

Education shows an even stronger effect: Developed nations average 12.1 years of schooling versus 7.4 years for developing nations â€” a 4.7-year education gap.

Key Insight

Education is the most modifiable factor. Unlike GDP, schooling years can be directly targeted through policy intervention.

Education Impact

Each additional year of education correlates with approximately 2 years of added life expectancy.

03 â€” Health Indicators

Healthcare Saves Lives

Mortality rates, vaccination coverage, and nutritional status directly impact how long populations survive. These are the proximal causes of longevity differences.

Mortality & Survival

Adult mortality (deaths per 1000 aged 15-60) shows a strong inverse relationship with life expectancy. The global average adult mortality is 163.7 per 1000, while infant mortality averages 23.56 per 1000.

Countries with high adult mortality also tend to have high infant mortality, suggesting systemic healthcare deficits affect all age groups.

Key Insight

Reducing adult mortality by 100 points (per 1,000) correlates with approximately 5 additional years of life expectancy.

Vaccination Coverage

Vaccination coverage tells a success story: Polio (88.3%), Diphtheria (87.9%), Hepatitis B (87.1%), and Measles (80.2%) coverage have all improved significantly from 2000-2015.

Higher vaccination rates correlate strongly with lower infant mortality and higher overall life expectancy.

Vaccination Success

Global vaccination rates improved across all four major vaccines from 2000-2015, contributing to reduced child mortality worldwide.

BMI & Nutritional Status

Body Mass Index (BMI) and thinness rates reveal nutritional determinants of health. Countries with higher average BMI (indicating adequate nutrition) generally have higher life expectancy, though extremes in either direction pose risks.

Thinness rates among children (5-9 years) and adolescents (10-19 years) are concentrated in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa.

Nutrition Impact

Countries with adequate nutrition (moderate BMI) show better health outcomes than those with high thinness rates indicating malnutrition.

06 â€” Key Findings

What the Data Reveals

Our analysis of 179 countries over 16 years reveals clear pathways to improving human longevity across the globe.

Education is Key

Each additional year of schooling correlates with ~2 years of added life expectancy. The 4.7-year education gap (12.1 vs 7.4 years) between developed and developing nations is a primary driver of health inequality.

Wealth Has Limits

GDP matters most for poor nations. Beyond $20,000 per capita, additional wealth provides diminishing returns for life expectancy â€” the Preston Curve in action.

Mortality Reduction Works

Countries that reduced adult mortality by 200+ points per 1000 gained the most life years. Basic healthcare access and disease prevention are transformative.

Africa is Catching Up

All top 15 improvers are African nations. Rwanda's 18.9-year gain proves that rapid improvement is possible with focused investment in health infrastructure.

Vaccination Saves Lives

Global vaccination rates for Polio (88.3%), Diphtheria (87.9%), Hepatitis B (87.1%), and Measles (80.2%) have significantly reduced child mortality worldwide.

The 12-Year Gap Persists

Despite progress, developed nations (78.51 years) still outlive developing nations (66.34 years) by over a decade. Closing this gap requires sustained global investment.

The Path Forward

The data reveals a clear path: investing in education, expanding healthcare access, and supporting economic development in the poorest nations can add decades to human life. The question isn't whether we can close the 37-year gap â€” it's whether we will.
